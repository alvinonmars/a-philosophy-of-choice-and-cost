Stones Beneath the Current

Preface

Many years ago, I wanted to package this book as an "ancient lost manuscript" that I had stumbled upon by chance. I didn't believe anyone would take seriously the musings of a young nobody writing about "choice" and "cost." Even the phrase "人选祸，天选福 (Human Choice brings Cost; Heaven's Selection brings Fortune)" was crafted to be catchy, to spread more easily — and so the cost came due. The story between you and me begins with my own act of "choosing misfortune for the sake of fortune."

My father was a man of letters. Under his influence, I read voraciously from a young age. By fifteen, I was already contemplating writing a book of my own. At the time, I wondered: why are so many people willing to study the Heart Sutra, the Diamond Sutra, and the Dao De Jing, while so few bother with Zhuangzi or the Nirvana Sutra? I concluded that "ease of transmission" is the key to whether an idea gains acceptance. The Heart Sutra and the Diamond Sutra are short — easy to memorize, easy to copy. The Dao De Jing opens with "The Dao that can be spoken is not the eternal Dao" — it rolls off the tongue, easy to read and recite. Zhuangzi, on the other hand — even now, I still can't pronounce many of the characters in a given passage. It was for the sake of easy transmission that I coined the catchy mantra "人选祸天选福 (Human Choice, Cost; Heaven's Selection, Fortune)," which led to a great many misunderstandings, and which in turn led directly to me sitting at my computer typing these words — forcing Stones Beneath the Current into a premature birth. Let me begin by apologizing to those I have misled.

With that in mind, I will not present this book in the form of classical Chinese text, nor will I rush to show you its value. Let us simply begin by getting to know my subjective world — consider this an invitation to step inside.

In my subjective view, the world operates according to laws. The universe is deterministic. The quantum leap of electrons, the structure of crystals, the motion of planets, chemical reactions, human evolution — none of these are the result of choices. They are the inevitable outcomes of natural law. In other words, from the very moment the universe was born, everything was already written. Time is merely the order in which we turn the pages. Time is nothing more than a ruler for measuring the phenomenon of change. Human thought is no different — neural responses, genes — all merely manifestations of these same laws.

Strictly speaking, in a materialist world, humans have no free will either. The fact that I am writing this preface to Stones Beneath the Current right now, and that you are reading these bewildering words — all of it was predetermined.

And yet, after deep reflection, one must "pretend" to have the power of choice. Because if you truly believed that everything was already determined, why would you strive? Why would you give anything up? Why would you choose at all? So we must live in a kind of simple state — knowing full well that everything may be determined, yet still acting as "the one who chooses." Just as we are born to die, yet we do not abandon the will to live.

Many people misunderstand "Human Choice brings Cost, Heaven's Selection brings Fortune" as fortune-telling. In truth, I do not believe the future can be predicted or interfered with. This brings me to my 破画原理 (The Painting Principle): no matter how strong a figure inside a painting may be, he cannot tear the canvas from within. In scientific terms, observation itself is part of the variable.

This is the obverse of my coin: the materialist world.

Now let us look at the reverse: the idealist world.

In my heart, no object truly exists in this world — it is only a projection of things within the mind. Let me illustrate. You are reading Stones Beneath the Current right now. You roughly know that I am still alive, what I look like, and that I will probably be livestreaming tonight. All of this exists within your mind — it does not truly exist in the world. Suppose one day, just before a livestream, I was struck and killed by a car. No one would ever update my content again, and I would slowly be forgotten. But then, during some chance moment of decision, you think of me. You might guess that I went into seclusion, that I became disillusioned with the world. In that moment, I am alive in your mind. Until one day you see the headline: "Famous remains blogger Luffy killed in car accident." In that moment, I die — I die within your mind. Of course, this could all be a story I staged myself while I sit in Antarctica watching penguins. And when one day you see a video of me petting sled dogs in Antarctica

, you are delighted — I am alive again in your mind. You decide the news report was wrong. But then you notice the tag in the corner: "Video suspected AI-generated." I die in your mind once more. Perhaps I was the one who Photoshopped that label on there myself.

The idealist world is vast. The materialist world and the idealist world are two sides of the same coin — they exist simultaneously but can never be observed at the same time. All right, this section is quite mind-bending, but it had to be said. The chapters that follow will go into specifics. As a preface, my only aim is to give you a rough sense of what this book is about.

From these two subjective worlds, a subjective principle emerges — and it is the very purpose that "Human Choice brings Cost, Heaven's Selection brings Fortune" hopes to achieve: you cannot determine the outcome of your life, but you can choose what cost to bear. We cannot avoid suffering, nor can we guarantee getting what we want. Life is not about gaining something or losing something. What we need to build is a structure that can sustain stable survival over the long term — something close to what I have always pursued: a good ending.

Person A has made a fortune, but lives every day in anxiety, 恐惧 (Fear), and insomnia. He has "gained" a great deal, but his structure is unstable. Person B earns an ordinary income, but his life is orderly, his relationships stable. He has not "gained" much, but his structure is stable. It is a crude example, but what I want to express is this: in my view, a stable structure takes priority over everything.

Over these years, I have met many people who are lost and suffering. Some are poor; some are financially free. Some have read many books; some have done many things. Some are beautiful; some are ugly. But they all suffer. Through observation, I have become more certain: suffering is not the exclusive domain of any particular type of person. It is something all people must face — all beings suffer.

The root causes of suffering fall into two broad categories: "not getting what you want" and "having already lost what you failed to cherish."

When I was sixteen, Wenjie and I secretly took money from our parents and ran off to Shennongjia for a trip. Muyu Village was beautiful, and we were strolling along a stream. The water was crystal clear, and the stones looked extraordinary. But when I crouched down to look, I could see the water yet could not see the stones clearly. The current was swift; water tumbled over the stones, and the surface heaved, glinted, and warped endlessly. The water looked shallow. I wanted to reach in and grab a stone from the bottom. I got my hand halfway in and immediately pulled it back — the water was bitterly cold. In that moment, it felt as though even my thoughts had frozen.

The next day we returned to the stream. Seeing that I was afraid of the cold, Wenjie went in and retrieved a stone from the bottom for me. The moment I saw him lift it out, the stone was not nearly as beautiful as I had imagined.

贪婪 (Greed) — wanting to take the beautiful stone. 恐惧 (Fear) — recoiling from the cold. Having already lost — the stone's mysterious beauty. Not getting what you want — the stone you envisioned in your mind.

Thoughts immediately swirled within me. I asked Wenjie not to disturb me for a while, and I began to think through the relationships at play, writing it all into my journal. That journal entry was long. It covered the relationship between rivers and knowledge, the speed of thought and the efficiency of thinking, methods for observing one's own 贪婪 (Greed) and 恐惧 (Fear), excuses, 自治 (Self-rationalization), and more.

I likened the 贪婪 (Greed) and 恐惧 (Fear) in the human heart to two stones at the bottom of the river — one white, one black — and likened excuses and 自治 (Self-rationalization) to the chaotic surface currents churned up by those stones. Greed makes you reach in; Fear makes you pull back. Wanting to obtain — that is Greed. Fearing loss — that is Fear. So many of life's decisions are made between these two stones.

But the problem was never Greed, nor was it Fear. The problem is this: between the two, is there still a structure within which one can choose? That is 天权 (Celestial Balance). If Greed and Fear can still hold each other in check, a person can remain steady. If one side completely overwhelms the other, a person veers toward extremes and falls into pathological depression or anxiety. Very few people have only Greed left, or only Fear. Most people oscillate between the two.

And so I subjectively divided the human condition into five stages:

Lost — 天权 (Celestial Balance) has collapsed. One doubles down endlessly while retreating endlessly.

Seeking Fortune — 天权 (Celestial Balance) wavers. One chases advantage and avoids harm.

Seeing Clearly — 天权 (Celestial Balance) becomes visible. One can clearly see one's own choices, costs, and outcomes.

Holding Steady — 天权 (Celestial Balance) is under control. One understands that cost cannot be avoided in life, and actively chooses to bear it.

两忘 (Double Forgetting) — 天权 (Celestial Balance) dissolves. There is no Greed, no Fear — and so both are forgotten. 天权 (Celestial Balance) is no longer needed.

But no one stays in any single stage forever. The human heart is like water — it shifts with circumstance. A person who is lucid today may be foolish tomorrow. The moment you see through something, your own desires may drag you back in the very next instant. These stages are only snapshots of the present moment.

I know that right now you are trying to figure out which stage you are in, and wondering which stage Jiang Lan is in. The main text will address this. For now, I can only say: I dare not, and have not earned the right, to step into 两忘 (Double Forgetting). At present, I even doubt whether anyone can truly reach it. This relates to something I have always said — using the biggest hook, the biggest bait, to catch the biggest fish.

Most people are Seeking Fortune. Wherever there is benefit, they go; wherever there is risk, they retreat. They appear very clever. But in my subjective world, the world never rewards anyone. The world only collects costs. Every choice you make demands a cost. Some are paid immediately. Some are not settled until many years later.

Many people believe there is an invoice between misfortune and fortune — a ledger of cause and effect. Endure a little now, and you will be repaid later. Take a small loss, and fate will compensate you. But the world is not a bank. Between misfortune and fortune, there is no ledger you can go back and audit.

And so, over time, I distilled many things into three words:

Human Choice (人选).

Cost (祸).

Heaven's Selection (天选).

Human Choice (人选) is the choice you make in the present moment. In the world of Human Choice, there are only two forces: the Human and Heaven. The Human is yourself; Heaven is every variable beyond yourself. Cost (祸) is not disaster — Cost is the price you pay. Heaven's Selection (天选) is the outcome. You can decide your choice, but the outcome never belongs to you alone.

You may be wondering: where did Fortune (福) go? Fortune is a stable structure — that will be explained in the main text.

Many people like to explain all of this with a single word — "luck." But "luck" makes it far too easy to stop thinking, to self-justify (自洽). In my view, much of what we call luck is nothing more than an accumulation of choices you cannot see. What you can determine is your own choice. Every choice made by everything other than you — that is Heaven's Selection (天选), and Heaven's Selection is unknowable. Countless choices interweave, ultimately producing an outcome. You cannot see the full path, so you call it luck.

And so, throughout this book, I will repeat one deeply subjective statement: there is no luck in this world — only choices.

Of course, if you go deeper still, you encounter an even larger question. If everything in the universe operates within natural law, if human consciousness itself may be nothing more than part of the equation, then what does "choice" even mean? I do not shy away from this. I am even willing to admit: perhaps we really are nothing more than figures inside a painting.

This brings me back to "breaking the painting." You cannot escape "fate," but you must still take responsibility for this one life. Many people spend their entire lives trying to avoid suffering, yet they do not realize: suffering cannot be avoided. Joy and suffering are, absolutely, split half and half. The only thing you can decide is how you are willing to bear it.

So this book does not teach people how to succeed, nor does it teach people how to attain "Fortune." It is not about gaining something, nor about avoiding loss. It is about building a structure — a structure that will not easily collapse, even as the world endlessly changes.

We cannot pursue eternity, because eternity itself is an illusion. What is truly stable has never been that which does not change, but that which can withstand change.

If this book has any value at all, I hope it is not that it helps you win against anyone, but that the next time you stand at the edge of that river, you see a little more clearly than before: why you reached in, why you pulled back, why you chose, and why you bore the cost.

If one day you can finally see those two stones at the bottom of your own river, then this book was probably not written in vain.

All right, dear reader. The collision of our inner worlds begins here.

Jiang Lan

Chapter 1: The River World

In my mind, there are many ways of seeing the world. I call them Small Worlds (小世界) — you can think of them as interpretive frameworks, not unlike the way an AI structures its understanding. The first time I shared my Small Worlds and my worldview with Jason, he got excited and told me this was essentially the underlying logic behind eighty percent of AI. The earliest of these Small Worlds — the very first one I ever built — was the "River World" (河流世界). It has had an enormous impact on my life and, later, on my career.

Wenjie and I were fifteen when we first wanted to join the army — to fulfill our ambitions and serve our country. The year we turned sixteen, figuring that the military physical exam might come the following year, we told our families we were going to Mount Wudang to study martial arts. Instead, we secretly went to Shennongjia to have an adventure. Before we set out, we each defined a goal for the trip: in those ten days, I would find the raw ore of He Shi Bi (和氏壁) — the legendary jade — and he would quit smoking. He'd been smoking since he was twelve. After that trip to Shennongjia, he almost never smoked again.

I never found my He Shi Bi. But I did push open the door to a Small World of independent thinking about the patterns of the world. A note here: the vocabulary I use probably comes from reading too many xianxia novels like Shrouding the Heavens (遮天) and Sword Come (剑来). There is absolutely no mysticism intended. I am personally opposed to all mysticism.

Alright. The story begins with a small river.

One morning, in Muyu Town, we each bought a box of doupi — a Wuhan-style bean curd wrap — and carried them down to the riverside to eat breakfast. The stream was very clear; so clear that standing on the bank and looking down, you'd think you could see the bottom. But if you looked carefully, you'd realize: you could see the bottom, but you couldn't see it clearly.

Legend has it that He Shi Bi came from the land of Chu — it was either turquoise or moonstone. The water flowed, and the stones sat beneath it, holding the current. The surface kept churning, reflecting, distorting — flashing with that blue light unique to moonstone and labradorite. I knew the stones were down there, but I couldn't see what they truly looked like. That day, staring at that river, I suddenly entered a very strange state. It hit me all at once: a person's thoughts are actually like a river. Not just like a river — they practically are one. Later, I slowly matched it up, piece by piece.

The water in the river is a person's flowing thoughts.

The water source is the information constantly flowing in from the outside world.

The riverbed is a person's innate nature, experiences, and the bedrock of their character.

The stones on the riverbed are their Greed (贪婪) and Fear (恐惧).

The width of the river is the breadth of their knowledge.

The depth of the water is the density of their knowledge.

The speed of the current is the speed of their thinking.

The riverbanks are the boundary of what a person knows they don't know.

The ripples on the surface are excuses, justifications, Self-rationalization (自治), and surface-level emotions.

Sounds far-fetched? Let me give you three examples, and it will all click.

Take a young person who scrolls Douyin all day. They know a little about a lot of things — a bit of finance today, a bit of history tomorrow, some relationship advice the day after, then a bit about entrepreneurship; a little philosophy here, a little military affairs there. You'd think this person has a very broad information base. And yes — their river is very wide. But the problem is, the water is very shallow. They know a bit of everything but nothing deeply. So even a few small stones — Greed and Fear — are enough to churn the surface into chaos. One day they're fired up, the next day they're in tears. Then they become stubbornly certain: today they believe life should be this way, tomorrow they think it should be that way. It's not that these people lack thoughts — quite the opposite, they have too many thoughts, too scattered, too fast. They have the breadth of knowledge but none of the density. The river is wide, the water is shallow, and the flow is heavy, so the surface is never calm — nothing but emotions, Self-rationalization, and excuses.

Now take an oncology professor. They don't need to know everything or follow every field, but in one or two directions they've dug very deep. This person's river doesn't need to be especially wide, but the water is deep. When the water is deep, even if there are stones on the bottom, they can't easily churn the surface into chaos. So this kind of person doesn't usually experience wild highs and lows — not because they have no emotions, and not because they have no Greed or Fear, but because their water is deep enough.

Then there's a friend of mine, a teacher named Chen Changwen. He has fourteen million followers on Weibo. His information range is extremely broad — he reads widely, sees a lot, knows a lot. A betrayal once cost him an enormous fortune. After that, he became deeply disillusioned with human nature and gradually withdrew from people. He stopped letting so much outside information into his inner world. He writes his own things, writes about his own life. More and more, it looks like he's deliberately reducing the flow. This kind of person is fascinating. His river was originally very wide, and the water wasn't shallow either. But later, he began to actively control where the water comes from and where it goes. So his surface appears calmer now — not because the world got quieter, but because he stopped letting so much turbulent water flood in.

So even though the metaphor is always "thoughts are like a river," different people are entirely different kinds of rivers:

Some are wide but shallow.

Some are narrow but deep.

Some are wide and deep, but the current is too fast.

Some had a beautiful river to begin with, but the flow became so heavy that the whole thing turned to chaos.

If you want to see yourself clearly, the first step is not to rush into asking what you're greedy for or what you fear. The first step is to figure out: what kind of river are you? What kind of river are you. The question sounds simple, but most people go their entire lives without ever seriously considering it. Every day, they watch the surface: today I'm in a bad mood, today I'm anxious, today I suddenly feel motivated, today I suddenly don't want to do anything. They assume the problem is their emotions, so they start trying to control their emotions. Some go to motivational seminars, some study psychology, some try meditation, some chase self-help gurus. But after all that effort, many of them end up right back where they started. Not because those methods are completely useless, but because they've been spinning on the surface the whole time. The surface will always move:

A gust of wind makes it move. Too much water makes it move. A faster current makes it move.

If you only stare at the surface, you'll always feel like the problems are endless. But when you step back and look at the whole river, you'll find that many things are actually very simple. Some people feel anxious not because they're too sensitive, but because their river is too shallow — the smallest thing hits bottom. Some people go around in circles, trapped in overthinking, not because they can't figure things out, but because their river is too narrow — every problem gets crammed into one tiny channel and churns endlessly. Some people swing between passion and despair not because their personality is contradictory, but because their flow is too heavy — information pours into their heart like a downpour, and the whole river never gets a chance to slow down. Some people have every advantage and still feel like their life is a mess, not because the world has singled them out, but because their current is too fast — the water keeps rushing, and things haven't had time to settle before the next wave carries them away.

So over time, I came to feel that most people make life far too complicated. They're always looking for "the answer" — one method that will solve everything. But in reality, many problems were already decided much further upstream.

What determines the shape of your surface is not the surface — it's the river.

If you don't look at the river first, all your effort may be aimed at the wrong place.

Some people frantically pour more water into their river, thinking that knowing more will solve their problems. All it does is make the surface more chaotic.

Some people desperately try to slow the current, but the river is too shallow — even at a crawl, it's still turbulent.

Some people try to block off a patch of the surface, never realizing that the real problem lies on the riverbed.

So over time, I developed a very simple habit: when I see someone whose life is in disarray, I don't immediately think about their emotions. I think about one thing first — what does their river look like? Is it too narrow, or too shallow? Is there too much water, or is it flowing too fast? Because once you see that clearly, half the problem is already solved. But when I kept thinking deeper, a new question appeared:

If a person's thoughts flow like river water, constantly moving —

then where does all that water actually go?

Between people, of course there is exchange — you hear me say a sentence, I read a book you wrote, we talk through the night, and water flows from one river into another. But if it were only this kind of exchange, it would actually disappear very quickly. Many thoughts don't exist only between two people. Some thoughts live for a very long time — some even survive for hundreds of years. Why? Because humanity has actually been doing one thing all along:

Making containers for river water.

The earliest container was the story. Then came the written word.

Then came books.

Then came art.

Now the container is called a large language model — "AI." All of these things are actually doing the same work: capturing the water from a river. A book is really a stretch of river that has been preserved. A person's decades of thinking, a lifetime of observation — if it's only spoken aloud in conversation, it will vanish quickly. But once it's written into a book, that river has been poured into a container. Hundreds of years later, someone will still open it and let that river flow into their own heart once more.

Art is the same.

A painting.

A piece of music.

A film.

Each one is a fragment of someone's river of thought,

solidified into a shape.

When someone else draws close, the river begins to flow again. History is even more so. History is not a pile of dates. History is really the shadow of countless riverbeds — the thoughts, choices, desires, and fears of countless people, compressed by time into the beds they left behind.

So gradually I came to realize something: the reason humanity has been able to keep advancing is not that each generation starts from zero, but that the rivers that came before never truly disappeared — they were poured into containers. When a person reads a book, they are really letting water from centuries ago flow into their own river. When a person studies history, they are really looking at riverbeds that have long since gone dry. When a person encounters art, they are really touching the flow that once moved through another river.

My river's surface is broad and calm, and that comes from the knowledge of the ancient sages — it didn't appear out of thin air. Many people mock me for wanting to be a "sage." I have never thought any such thing. What the sages did was originate and innovate. What I do is inherit and carry forward. I know I have received an enormous gift, and with it I have incurred an enormous debt. I have both the obligation and the responsibility, before I leave this world, to gather my river into a container and give it back. Even if it returns just one percent in interest, that would be a life well-spent. So I joke that I'm a bankrupt man, repaying a debt — and a debtor has no right to ask others not to raise the price or to wait for him.

So when I later started writing, I was also thinking about a question: if I write my river into a book, am I letting all the water out? Will my river one day run dry? Over time, I realized it doesn't work that way. Because when water is poured into a container, it doesn't vanish — it simply takes on a different form of existence. The water in a book flows to others, and their rivers produce new water, which flows back again. Thought is never one-directional. It's more like an entire system — countless rivers exchanging, branching, merging, scouring each other's beds.

So when a person begins to express themselves, they are not draining their river. Quite the opposite — they are setting the whole river into true motion. And once a river begins to flow at speed, the riverbed starts to be scoured. Things that once seemed solid get carried away. Things that were once invisible are revealed. The river grows deeper. But as the river deepens, you also begin to see things you couldn't see before — the things that truly change the direction of the current, the things that have always been down there, yet are rarely seen.

That day, beside that little stream in Shennongjia, I hadn't fully worked all of this out. But I had begun to sense something: if a person truly wants to see themselves clearly, it's not enough to look at the water. It's not even enough to look at the river. Because the things that truly change the direction of the current have always been below — on the riverbed.

This is the next story in the River World: "The Stones of Greed and Fear."

Chapter 2: The Path of Awakening — The Way of Man

The next morning, Wenjie and I went back to that little river. I knew perfectly well that the stone was down there. I just couldn't see its true form. The day before, I had wanted to fish it out, because underwater it gave off a very strange kind of light. Not the dull, dry reflection of an ordinary stone, but a hazy blue — much like moonstone, a cold blue glimmering through a pale white base. At the time, I even thought it might be something truly special — a stone like He Shi Bi (和氏壁), the legendary jade. I crouched down and reached into the water, but the instant my fingers touched it, I pulled my hand right back. It was freezing.

I wanted it badly, but I wasn't willing to pay the price. So the next day, when Wenjie saw me still staring at it, he laughed, jumped straight into the water, then actually lay down and thrust his hand in, grabbed around a few times, and pulled the stone out. The moment he held it up in front of me, I froze — because it looked nothing like what I had imagined.

No blue glow. No sense of mystery. None of that feeling that "something must be hidden inside." It was just an ordinary stone — a grayish-white base with some fine flakes of mica catching bits of light.

I stood there by that little river, next to a rotting pine log, staring at the stone for a long time. My first reaction wasn't disappointment — it was to start constructing reasons. I wondered if it was the refraction of the water, if maybe the mica flakes had their blue stretched and amplified by the surface and the current when the stone was submerged. The more I thought about it, the more reasonable these explanations seemed. So I carefully placed the stone back in the water. I wanted to see if it would emit that blue glow again. But it didn't.

Once it was back in the water, it never returned to what I had seen the first time. Standing there, I suddenly understood something: that blue light had never been on the stone, nor in the water. It had only ever existed inside my mind.

All those explanations I had desperately pieced together weren't a search for truth — they were an effort to prove that the cause-and-effect I had originally perceived was correct. Over time, I became more and more certain: this is what people do. We see a result first, then scramble to find a cause, then find evidence to support that cause.

In the end, we convince ourselves: I wasn't wrong, I was right, the world really is this way. But so often, all you have to do is put the stone back in the water and look again, and those reasons shatter on their own.

It was discovering the power of repeated observation and comparison that gave birth to this book, Stones Beneath the Current.

Recording is crucial. Because only through recording can you preserve what you were actually thinking at the time. Then, after some time has passed, you look back — and only then do you know whether what you were seeing was the stone on the riverbed or just ripples on the surface. I ended up bringing that stone home. Because of it, I even gave up Wenjie's secondhand Sony Ericsson music phone — I remember clearly it was a w800c. To this day, the stone still sits on my desk. It looks like nothing more than an ordinary rock, but I believe it matters more than most things, because so much of my later thinking about human nature actually began at that little riverside. If you ever see it someday, you probably wouldn't think there's anything special about it.

But for me, it is where everything began.

It was also from that point on that I seriously considered something for the first time: if a person's mind really is a river, then how exactly do I observe my own?

During the period that followed, I started writing in my journal every day. I wrote earnestly, and after finishing, I would even show it to Wenjie. He would give me very honest reactions. After reading, he would understand me a little better.

But one day, he suddenly told me: "Don't show me your journal anymore."

That day, we were crossing the intersection below Danjiangkou City First People's Hospital, on our way to Shentong Internet Café to play CrossFire. I stopped for a moment and asked him why. He said something I have remembered for many years.

He said: "What people express to others is always, in some way, dishonest."

I didn't understand at the time. He told me that his parents divorced when he was young, and he grew up with his grandparents. His father would often badmouth his mother. His mother would often tell him his father was cruel.

He said: "Nothing anyone says is entirely a lie, but nothing is entirely the truth either. Because whenever anyone speaks, they have their own purpose in mind, their own things they want to protect, and their own version of the story they want others to believe."

Then he looked at me and said: "So as long as you're writing for someone else to read, it's already not real."

When I heard that, I was actually furious. I had genuinely wanted to share my reflections on life with him. I felt like he was dismissing me. I had written those things with complete sincerity — I even felt they were a real part of me. And then he just says, "It's all dishonest."

I absolutely could not accept it. We had a fight over it. After that, for about two months, we stubbornly refused to contact each other. During that time, I didn't show my journal to anyone. Later, I went back and reread what I had written before, and I realized he was right. When I had been writing with the intention of showing it to him, I would unconsciously arrange my language, unconsciously emphasize certain emotions, and unconsciously hide certain things.

Now, if I were to write it again, I wouldn't write it that way. In that moment, I finally understood. What he said was true. As soon as something is written for another person's eyes, it has already begun to distort. From that day on, I never showed my journal to anyone again. But writing this for you today — that too will inevitably involve some distortion. I'm doing my best to control it, but the very effort to control the distortion only causes the distortion to shift further.

Are you ready now? Ready to enter the world of my river and walk your own path of intellectual awakening — the Way of Man?

Let's begin:

If the human mind is a river, then seeing clearly to the bottom is actually very difficult — because the water never stops flowing.

Information flows.

Emotions flow.

Desires flow.

Many people spend their entire lives seeing only the surface — the excuses, the reasons, the disguises — and never once see the riverbed. Gradually, I came to the conclusion that there are roughly three ways to see the stones at the bottom.

The first is called Fishing for Stones (捞石头) — keeping a daily Dao Marks (道痕) journal.

The second is called Diving (潜水) — deep contemplation and meditation.

The third is called Redirecting the River (改河道) — completely dissolving the former self.

But for the vast majority of people, I only recommend starting with the first method. Don't underestimate the Way of Man — its destination converges with the Way of Heaven. And that method is: Fishing for Stones. Because it is the slowest, but also the safest, and costs the least.

What does Fishing for Stones mean? It means you don't rush to see through the entire river at once. You just fish out one stone. Today, fish out one. Tomorrow, fish out another. Pull it up, look at it, put it back, look again, and write it down.

What you want is not to see every stone in the river all at once. Nor is it to completely transform yourself today. What you want is to slowly build a capacity:

To see the surface of the water and know, roughly, what lies beneath.

To see the surface of the water and know, roughly, what lies beneath.

This is the seed of Celestial Insight (天机). This is not mysticism, and it is certainly not fortune-telling. It is your subconscious ability to read your own riverbed with increasing accuracy. So how do you fish?

I eventually found a very simple method for myself: write in my journal every day at my most exhausted moment. At the time, I already loved exercise — working out, running every day, keeping my body in good shape. Later I discovered that the period right after a run was especially suited for Fishing for Stones. Because after a run, your body is already spent. Blood and oxygen flow to your limbs and throughout your body, and your brain actually stops running at its usual breakneck speed.

At that point, the river slows down. Not a complete stop, but a noticeable slowing. And when the water slows, the surface calms. And when it calms, it becomes easier to see the bottom. So the method I settled on for myself was:

Run.

Shower.

Then write.

How you write matters enormously. When I used to guide my apprentices in journaling, most of them would write things like: "Today was exhausting," "Today I was unhappy," "Today someone disappointed me," "Today I figured something out." These entries have very little value as records. They are all just ripples on the surface. What we need to record is: Dao Marks.

Not a record of your life.

Not a record of your feelings.

Not a record of your stories.

But rather, fishing out one stone from the river each day. How do you write in a way that isn't just skimming the surface? I suggest that every Dao Marks entry follow this fixed structure:

Layer One: The Facts

Start by writing only what happened. No explaining, no evaluating, no emoting. For example: "Today he said I've changed." "Today I saw someone else making money fast." "Today I meant to reply to a message but didn't." "Today I saw someone showing off and it made me uncomfortable." The purpose of this layer is to mark the location of the stone. Many people start by writing principles and lessons — that only buries the stone deeper.

Layer Two: The First Reaction

Write your most immediate, gut-level feeling. Not the "correct" answer — the first reaction. For example: "I wanted to argue back." "I felt jealous." "I was a bit scared." "I suddenly wanted to prove myself." "I wanted to run." This layer is critical, because the first reaction is usually the closest thing to the stone.

Layer Three: What do I want to get out of this? This step is about finding Greed (贪婪).

Force yourself to answer one thing: if I follow this impulse forward, what am I actually trying to get? And don't use big, abstract words. Don't write "dignity" or "happiness" or "meaning." Write something specific. For example: "I want him to admit I wasn't wrong." "I want to be better than that person." "I want others to think highly of me." "I want to keep this relationship." "I want to seize this opportunity." "I want to confirm that I'm not a failure." Once you write this step, Greed usually reveals itself.

Layer Four: What am I afraid of?

This step is about finding Fear (恐惧). Again, no big words. Write the specific, smallest, most direct fear. For example: "I'm afraid of being looked down on." "I'm afraid I'm not actually that talented." "I'm afraid of losing them." "I'm afraid I chose wrong." "I'm afraid that admitting this would make me look stupid." "I'm afraid I've been lying to myself all along." This step is especially important, because many people can only write about what they greedily want — they can't write about what they fear. But what truly makes a person pull back their hand is usually found here.

Layer Five: What excuse did I make for myself?

This is the most critical step. This is about finding the ripples on the surface. Ask yourself: to feel a little more comfortable, what explanations did I construct for this situation? For example: "I'm not jealous — I'm just being objective." "I'm not afraid — I just want to wait a little longer." "It's not that I don't want it — I just see further ahead." "I'm not angry — I just don't think it's worth it." "I'm not trying to prove myself — I'm just speaking fairly." What this layer records is: how you engaged in Self-rationalization (自治). Many people stop their journals at the third layer. Because the real stones are usually hidden behind the reasons you're best at articulating. So never stop Fishing for Stones — the real stones are always hiding behind your most eloquent justifications.

Layer Six: What stone did I fish out today?

At the end, you are only allowed to write a single conclusion: Today the stone I fished out is: ________. Write only one main stone. Don't get greedy and fearful and list a whole pile. For example: "Today's main stone is: fear of being undervalued." "Today's main stone is: wanting to prove I'm stronger." "Today's main stone is: wanting certainty." "Today's main stone is: fear of losing a relationship." "Today's main stone is: fear of admitting I was wrong." This step is critical, because it forces you to move from a mass of surface ripples down to a single stone.

Why do so many people find journaling useless? Because they only write the first three layers: what happened, how I felt.

"Today I felt wronged." Sure, that has some value — but it's only "seeing the surface." Anyone who truly learns to see themselves will always write two more layers: what do I actually want, and what am I actually afraid of?

Then add one more: what explanation did I fabricate to protect myself?

When you write to this point, you finally begin to actually touch the stone. And why must you go back and reread? You've already intuited this yourself — and that's the most powerful thing about it. Fishing out a stone isn't about collecting it. It's about comparison.

You fish it out and look at it today. Tomorrow you put it back and look again. A week later, look again. A month later, look again. You will discover three things:

First, what you thought was a big deal at the time turns out not to have been that stone at all.

Second, what you thought you saw so clearly was actually nothing but Self-rationalization.

Third, certain stones keep reappearing.

This third point is the most important. If a stone keeps showing up, it isn't a passing emotion — it is one of the great stones on your riverbed. So my recommendation is: write every day, reread once a week, and each month identify only "the three stones that keep recurring." After one month, you will be shocked. Because you will discover that the life you thought was so complex is actually being pushed along, over and over, by just a few stones.

How do you use journaling to learn Human Choice (人选)? This is the real point. Many people think that "seeing the stone" is enough. It isn't. Seeing the stone is only the first step. True Human Choice is when the next time you encounter the same ripple, you know which stone lies beneath it.

For example, before, when you saw someone else succeed, it would stir up waves. You would only feel a vague discomfort. But after ten days of writing Dao Marks, you would know: "Ah, this isn't just discomfort — underneath it is the desire to prove I'm stronger." Or: "Ah, this isn't rational hesitation — underneath it is the fear of bearing the cost."

At that point, you slowly begin to earn the right to "choose." But how to actually choose — that will probably have to wait until around Chapter 30. Shortly after I published the original Chapter 1, many people couldn't wait to start choosing, and that genuinely frightened me. They didn't want to build the foundation, didn't want to hear the principles — they just wanted to jump straight to choosing. That only made me more determined to place the methodology of choosing much further along in the book.

The Dao Marks template — you can use this directly, one page per day:

1. What was the one thing today that stirred the most waves in me?

2. What was my first reaction at the time?

3. What did I actually want to get?

4. What was I actually afraid of?

5. What excuse did I make for myself?

6. What is today's main stone?

7. If the same thing happens tomorrow, how do I plan to choose?

The first six questions are about examining the stone. The seventh is where you begin to simulate Human Choice. Some people have criticized my theory of Human Choice and Celestial Selection as mysticism. After reading the preface, they realized it wasn't. Now they've shifted to calling it empty platitudes — unfalsifiable, unprovable. After finishing this chapter, let's wait and see. Stones Beneath the Current is a highly practical guide to action.

Keep fishing. Fish long enough, and you will slowly come to know what is water, what are ripples, what are stones, and what is you. And when you begin to know these things, only then have you truly earned your ticket of admission to studying the theory of Human Choice and Celestial Selection.

The second method is Diving,

which is to say, deep contemplation. This method is fast, and it is dangerous. I must be clear about the danger, because many people hear "deep contemplation" and assume it's something lofty and elevated. It isn't. It can very easily pull a person into the void. In my life, I have had two episodes that came close to depression, and both were related to Diving.

The first time, a friend who had been by my side for many years was diagnosed with liver cancer. During that period, I couldn't stop thinking about death. I thought too deeply, too broadly, and I nearly couldn't find my way out.

The second time, I was studying the structure of human genetics. I glimpsed something very fundamental, and I kept digging deeper. The further I dug, the more a person can fall into a kind of emptiness.

So I became increasingly certain: deep contemplation is not something everyone is ready to do immediately. If you don't yet know how to read your own river, can't yet distinguish the stones, can't yet judge your own current state — and you dive straight in — you can very easily send yourself into depression first. So in this chapter, I won't teach you how to Dive. At most, I'll tell you: it exists, it's fast, and it's dangerous. Once the structures I've described earlier become clearer to you, diving will be much safer.

The third method is Redirecting the River.

This is the most powerful, and the most difficult. Redirecting the River doesn't mean examining the stones — it means directly altering the river's course. Once a river truly changes course, the old riverbed is exposed for a time, and during that window, you can see the bottom with extraordinary clarity. So before you redirect, you should ideally already know what your original riverbed looks like. After the redirect, look again. Compare before and after — and the effect will be staggering.

But the cost of this method is enormous. I won't go into detail here, because the moment I do, many people will mistake it for mysticism again. Just remember one thing for now: Redirecting the River is powerful, but it is not something ordinary people should casually attempt.

If a person truly wants to know themselves, the first step is not deep contemplation, nor is it restructuring. The first step is to quietly fish out one stone. Then fish out one every day, bit by bit, comparing bit by bit, gradually correcting all those judgments that seemed so reasonable but were really just Self-rationalization.

You will slowly discover: why you reach out, why you pull back, why you keep falling for the same kind of "blue glow." And why, every time you pick it up, you find it's nothing more than an ordinary stone. When you begin to do this, you have truly started to understand your own inner world.

Twenty years have passed, and that stone still sits on my desk, next to my Yixing clay teapot. Beside it rests a blue Sri Lankan moonstone. Sometimes I pick them up and compare them. The river stone is really nothing special — grayish-white, with a faint shimmer of mica. If you saw it by a riverbank, you probably wouldn't give it a second glance. But every time I see it, I think of that little river, and I think of the person I was, standing at the water's edge all those years ago.

What set all of this in motion was nothing more than: Greed (贪婪) and Fear (恐惧).

In the next chapter, I'll take you into my world of "the two-sided coin." Are you ready? To walk through a forest of Greed and Fear — and emerge without a single petal clinging to you.
