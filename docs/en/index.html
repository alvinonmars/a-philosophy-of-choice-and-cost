Stones Beneath the Current

Preface

Many years ago, I wanted to package this book as an "ancient lost manuscript" that I had stumbled upon by chance. I didn't believe anyone would take seriously the musings of a young nobody writing about "choice" and "cost." Even the phrase "人选祸，天选福 (Human Choice brings Cost; Heaven's Selection brings Fortune)" was crafted to be catchy, to spread more easily — and so the cost came due. The story between you and me begins with my own act of "choosing misfortune for the sake of fortune."

My father was a man of letters. Under his influence, I read voraciously from a young age. By fifteen, I was already contemplating writing a book of my own. At the time, I wondered: why are so many people willing to study the Heart Sutra, the Diamond Sutra, and the Dao De Jing, while so few bother with Zhuangzi or the Nirvana Sutra? I concluded that "ease of transmission" is the key to whether an idea gains acceptance. The Heart Sutra and the Diamond Sutra are short — easy to memorize, easy to copy. The Dao De Jing opens with "The Dao that can be spoken is not the eternal Dao" — it rolls off the tongue, easy to read and recite. Zhuangzi, on the other hand — even now, I still can't pronounce many of the characters in a given passage. It was for the sake of easy transmission that I coined the catchy mantra "人选祸天选福 (Human Choice, Cost; Heaven's Selection, Fortune)," which led to a great many misunderstandings, and which in turn led directly to me sitting at my computer typing these words — forcing Stones Beneath the Current into a premature birth. Let me begin by apologizing to those I have misled.

With that in mind, I will not present this book in the form of classical Chinese text, nor will I rush to show you its value. Let us simply begin by getting to know my subjective world — consider this an invitation to step inside.

In my subjective view, the world operates according to laws. The universe is deterministic. The quantum leap of electrons, the structure of crystals, the motion of planets, chemical reactions, human evolution — none of these are the result of choices. They are the inevitable outcomes of natural law. In other words, from the very moment the universe was born, everything was already written. Time is merely the order in which we turn the pages. Time is nothing more than a ruler for measuring the phenomenon of change. Human thought is no different — neural responses, genes — all merely manifestations of these same laws.

Strictly speaking, in a materialist world, humans have no free will either. The fact that I am writing this preface to Stones Beneath the Current right now, and that you are reading these bewildering words — all of it was predetermined.

And yet, after deep reflection, one must "pretend" to have the power of choice. Because if you truly believed that everything was already determined, why would you strive? Why would you give anything up? Why would you choose at all? So we must live in a kind of simple state — knowing full well that everything may be determined, yet still acting as "the one who chooses." Just as we are born to die, yet we do not abandon the will to live.

Many people misunderstand "Human Choice brings Cost, Heaven's Selection brings Fortune" as fortune-telling. In truth, I do not believe the future can be predicted or interfered with. This brings me to my 破画原理 (The Painting Principle): no matter how strong a figure inside a painting may be, he cannot tear the canvas from within. In scientific terms, observation itself is part of the variable.

This is the obverse of my coin: the materialist world.

Now let us look at the reverse: the idealist world.

In my heart, no object truly exists in this world — it is only a projection of things within the mind. Let me illustrate. You are reading Stones Beneath the Current right now. You roughly know that I am still alive, what I look like, and that I will probably be livestreaming tonight. All of this exists within your mind — it does not truly exist in the world. Suppose one day, just before a livestream, I was struck and killed by a car. No one would ever update my content again, and I would slowly be forgotten. But then, during some chance moment of decision, you think of me. You might guess that I went into seclusion, that I became disillusioned with the world. In that moment, I am alive in your mind. Until one day you see the headline: "Famous remains blogger Luffy killed in car accident." In that moment, I die — I die within your mind. Of course, this could all be a story I staged myself while I sit in Antarctica watching penguins. And when one day you see a video of me petting sled dogs in Antarctica

, you are delighted — I am alive again in your mind. You decide the news report was wrong. But then you notice the tag in the corner: "Video suspected AI-generated." I die in your mind once more. Perhaps I was the one who Photoshopped that label on there myself.

The idealist world is vast. The materialist world and the idealist world are two sides of the same coin — they exist simultaneously but can never be observed at the same time. All right, this section is quite mind-bending, but it had to be said. The chapters that follow will go into specifics. As a preface, my only aim is to give you a rough sense of what this book is about.

From these two subjective worlds, a subjective principle emerges — and it is the very purpose that "Human Choice brings Cost, Heaven's Selection brings Fortune" hopes to achieve: you cannot determine the outcome of your life, but you can choose what cost to bear. We cannot avoid suffering, nor can we guarantee getting what we want. Life is not about gaining something or losing something. What we need to build is a structure that can sustain stable survival over the long term — something close to what I have always pursued: a good ending.

Person A has made a fortune, but lives every day in anxiety, 恐惧 (Fear), and insomnia. He has "gained" a great deal, but his structure is unstable. Person B earns an ordinary income, but his life is orderly, his relationships stable. He has not "gained" much, but his structure is stable. It is a crude example, but what I want to express is this: in my view, a stable structure takes priority over everything.

Over these years, I have met many people who are lost and suffering. Some are poor; some are financially free. Some have read many books; some have done many things. Some are beautiful; some are ugly. But they all suffer. Through observation, I have become more certain: suffering is not the exclusive domain of any particular type of person. It is something all people must face — all beings suffer.

The root causes of suffering fall into two broad categories: "not getting what you want" and "having already lost what you failed to cherish."

When I was sixteen, Wenjie and I secretly took money from our parents and ran off to Shennongjia for a trip. Muyu Village was beautiful, and we were strolling along a stream. The water was crystal clear, and the stones looked extraordinary. But when I crouched down to look, I could see the water yet could not see the stones clearly. The current was swift; water tumbled over the stones, and the surface heaved, glinted, and warped endlessly. The water looked shallow. I wanted to reach in and grab a stone from the bottom. I got my hand halfway in and immediately pulled it back — the water was bitterly cold. In that moment, it felt as though even my thoughts had frozen.

The next day we returned to the stream. Seeing that I was afraid of the cold, Wenjie went in and retrieved a stone from the bottom for me. The moment I saw him lift it out, the stone was not nearly as beautiful as I had imagined.

贪婪 (Greed) — wanting to take the beautiful stone. 恐惧 (Fear) — recoiling from the cold. Having already lost — the stone's mysterious beauty. Not getting what you want — the stone you envisioned in your mind.

Thoughts immediately swirled within me. I asked Wenjie not to disturb me for a while, and I began to think through the relationships at play, writing it all into my journal. That journal entry was long. It covered the relationship between rivers and knowledge, the speed of thought and the efficiency of thinking, methods for observing one's own 贪婪 (Greed) and 恐惧 (Fear), excuses, 自治 (Self-rationalization), and more.

I likened the 贪婪 (Greed) and 恐惧 (Fear) in the human heart to two stones at the bottom of the river — one white, one black — and likened excuses and 自治 (Self-rationalization) to the chaotic surface currents churned up by those stones. Greed makes you reach in; Fear makes you pull back. Wanting to obtain — that is Greed. Fearing loss — that is Fear. So many of life's decisions are made between these two stones.

But the problem was never Greed, nor was it Fear. The problem is this: between the two, is there still a structure within which one can choose? That is 天权 (Celestial Balance). If Greed and Fear can still hold each other in check, a person can remain steady. If one side completely overwhelms the other, a person veers toward extremes and falls into pathological depression or anxiety. Very few people have only Greed left, or only Fear. Most people oscillate between the two.

And so I subjectively divided the human condition into five stages:

Lost — 天权 (Celestial Balance) has collapsed. One doubles down endlessly while retreating endlessly.

Seeking Fortune — 天权 (Celestial Balance) wavers. One chases advantage and avoids harm.

Seeing Clearly — 天权 (Celestial Balance) becomes visible. One can clearly see one's own choices, costs, and outcomes.

Holding Steady — 天权 (Celestial Balance) is under control. One understands that cost cannot be avoided in life, and actively chooses to bear it.

两忘 (Double Forgetting) — 天权 (Celestial Balance) dissolves. There is no Greed, no Fear — and so both are forgotten. 天权 (Celestial Balance) is no longer needed.

But no one stays in any single stage forever. The human heart is like water — it shifts with circumstance. A person who is lucid today may be foolish tomorrow. The moment you see through something, your own desires may drag you back in the very next instant. These stages are only snapshots of the present moment.

I know that right now you are trying to figure out which stage you are in, and wondering which stage Jiang Lan is in. The main text will address this. For now, I can only say: I dare not, and have not earned the right, to step into 两忘 (Double Forgetting). At present, I even doubt whether anyone can truly reach it. This relates to something I have always said — using the biggest hook, the biggest bait, to catch the biggest fish.

Most people are Seeking Fortune. Wherever there is benefit, they go; wherever there is risk, they retreat. They appear very clever. But in my subjective world, the world never rewards anyone. The world only collects costs. Every choice you make demands a cost. Some are paid immediately. Some are not settled until many years later.

Many people believe there is an invoice between misfortune and fortune — a ledger of cause and effect. Endure a little now, and you will be repaid later. Take a small loss, and fate will compensate you. But the world is not a bank. Between misfortune and fortune, there is no ledger you can go back and audit.

And so, over time, I distilled many things into three words:

Human Choice (人选).

Cost (祸).

Heaven's Selection (天选).

Human Choice (人选) is the choice you make in the present moment. In the world of Human Choice, there are only two forces: the Human and Heaven. The Human is yourself; Heaven is every variable beyond yourself. Cost (祸) is not disaster — Cost is the price you pay. Heaven's Selection (天选) is the outcome. You can decide your choice, but the outcome never belongs to you alone.

You may be wondering: where did Fortune (福) go? Fortune is a stable structure — that will be explained in the main text.

Many people like to explain all of this with a single word — "luck." But "luck" makes it far too easy to stop thinking, to self-justify (自洽). In my view, much of what we call luck is nothing more than an accumulation of choices you cannot see. What you can determine is your own choice. Every choice made by everything other than you — that is Heaven's Selection (天选), and Heaven's Selection is unknowable. Countless choices interweave, ultimately producing an outcome. You cannot see the full path, so you call it luck.

And so, throughout this book, I will repeat one deeply subjective statement: there is no luck in this world — only choices.

Of course, if you go deeper still, you encounter an even larger question. If everything in the universe operates within natural law, if human consciousness itself may be nothing more than part of the equation, then what does "choice" even mean? I do not shy away from this. I am even willing to admit: perhaps we really are nothing more than figures inside a painting.

This brings me back to "breaking the painting." You cannot escape "fate," but you must still take responsibility for this one life. Many people spend their entire lives trying to avoid suffering, yet they do not realize: suffering cannot be avoided. Joy and suffering are, absolutely, split half and half. The only thing you can decide is how you are willing to bear it.

So this book does not teach people how to succeed, nor does it teach people how to attain "Fortune." It is not about gaining something, nor about avoiding loss. It is about building a structure — a structure that will not easily collapse, even as the world endlessly changes.

We cannot pursue eternity, because eternity itself is an illusion. What is truly stable has never been that which does not change, but that which can withstand change.

If this book has any value at all, I hope it is not that it helps you win against anyone, but that the next time you stand at the edge of that river, you see a little more clearly than before: why you reached in, why you pulled back, why you chose, and why you bore the cost.

If one day you can finally see those two stones at the bottom of your own river, then this book was probably not written in vain.

All right, dear reader. The collision of our inner worlds begins here.

Jiang Lan
