# Chapter 1: The River World | 第一章 · 河流世界

> 在我的心中有很多看待世界的方法，我称之为小世界，你可以理解他是一种ai的理解框架。我第一次和jason分享我的小世界的和世界观的时候，他很兴奋地和我说这就是百分之80的ai的底层思维。其中建立最早的第一个小世界就是"河流世界"，这对我的人生以及日后的事业产生了极大的影响。

In my mind, there are many ways of seeing the world. I call them Small Worlds (小世界) — you can think of them as interpretive frameworks, not unlike the way an AI structures its understanding. The first time I shared my Small Worlds and my worldview with Jason, he got excited and told me this was essentially the underlying logic behind eighty percent of AI. The earliest of these Small Worlds — the very first one I ever built — was the "River World" (河流世界). It has had an enormous impact on my life and, later, on my career.

---

> 我和文杰15岁就特想去当兵实现抱负，报效祖国，十六岁那年，考虑明年可能会去兵检了。我和文杰骗了家里的钱说是去武当山学武术，最后偷偷去神农架玩。我们出发前就定义了自己的目标：这10天我要找到和氏壁的原矿，他要把烟戒掉——他12岁就开始抽烟了，自去过神农架后几乎再没抽过烟。

Wenjie and I were fifteen when we first wanted to join the army — to fulfill our ambitions and serve our country. The year we turned sixteen, figuring that the military physical exam might come the following year, we told our families we were going to Mount Wudang to study martial arts. Instead, we secretly went to Shennongjia to have an adventure. Before we set out, we each defined a goal for the trip: in those ten days, I would find the raw ore of He Shi Bi (和氏壁) — the legendary jade — and he would quit smoking. He'd been smoking since he was twelve. After that trip to Shennongjia, he almost never smoked again.

---

> 我虽然没找到我的和氏壁，却打开了独立思考世界规律的小世界大门，在此提醒，我这些词语也许是来自于看多了《遮天》《剑来》这些玄幻小说的词汇，绝对没有玄学的意思，我本人反对一切玄学。

I never found my He Shi Bi. But I did push open the door to a Small World of independent thinking about the patterns of the world. A note here: the vocabulary I use probably comes from reading too many xianxia novels like *Shrouding the Heavens* (遮天) and *Sword Come* (剑来). There is absolutely no mysticism intended. I am personally opposed to all mysticism.

---

> 好吧，故事从一条小河开始。

Alright. The story begins with a small river.

---

> 有天一早，我们在木鱼镇一人买了一盒武汉的豆皮，我们拿着豆皮跑到河边吃早餐。那条溪水很清；清到你站在岸边往下看，会觉得自己已经看见了河底。可认真看，就会发现：你能看见底，却看不清底。

One morning, in Muyu Town, we each bought a box of doupi — a Wuhan-style bean curd wrap — and carried them down to the riverside to eat breakfast. The stream was very clear; so clear that standing on the bank and looking down, you'd think you could see the bottom. But if you looked carefully, you'd realize: you could see the bottom, but you couldn't see it clearly.

---

> 传说中的和氏壁在楚地，不是绿松石就是月光石，水在流，石头在底下顶着水，水面不断翻涌、反光、扭曲，闪着月光石拉长石独有的蓝光。我知道石头就在下面，却看不到它真正的样子。那天我看着那条河，突然进入了一种很奇怪的状态。我忽然觉得，人的思想其实就像一条河，不是像，几乎就是。后来我慢慢把这件事一条一条对上了。

Legend has it that He Shi Bi came from the land of Chu — it was either turquoise or moonstone. The water flowed, and the stones sat beneath it, holding the current. The surface kept churning, reflecting, distorting — flashing with that blue light unique to moonstone and labradorite. I knew the stones were down there, but I couldn't see what they truly looked like. That day, staring at that river, I suddenly entered a very strange state. It hit me all at once: a person's thoughts are actually like a river. Not just *like* a river — they practically *are* one. Later, I slowly matched it up, piece by piece.

---

> 河里的水，就是一个人流动的思想。
>
> 水源，就是外界不断流入的信息。
>
> 河床，就是一个人的天性、经历、性格底色。
>
> 河床上的石头，就是他的贪婪和恐惧。
>
> 河的宽度，就是知识的广度。
>
> 水的深度，就是知识的密度。
>
> 水流的速度，就是思考的速度。
>
> 河岸，是一个人知道自己不知道的知识边界。
>
> 水面的波纹，就是借口，理由，自治和表层情绪。

> The water in the river is a person's flowing thoughts.
>
> The water source is the information constantly flowing in from the outside world.
>
> The riverbed is a person's innate nature, experiences, and the bedrock of their character.
>
> The stones on the riverbed are their Greed (贪婪) and Fear (恐惧).
>
> The width of the river is the breadth of their knowledge.
>
> The depth of the water is the density of their knowledge.
>
> The speed of the current is the speed of their thinking.
>
> The riverbanks are the boundary of what a person knows they don't know.
>
> The ripples on the surface are excuses, justifications, Self-rationalization (自治), and surface-level emotions.

---

> 听起来很悬？我给你们三个例子，你们就会豁然开朗起来。

Sounds far-fetched? Let me give you three examples, and it will all click.

---

> 比如一个天天刷抖音的年轻人，他知道很多东西，今天一点财经，明天看一点历史，后天看一点情感，再往后看一点创业；一会看哲学，一会儿看军事，你会觉得这种人信息面很广。对，他的河很宽，但他的问题是，水很浅，什么都知道一点，什么都不深。于是河流多一点点石头（贪婪恐惧），就能把水面翻得乱七八糟；今天热血，明天泪目；明天又很固执：今天觉得人生该这样，明天又觉得人生应该那样，这种人不是没有思想，恰恰相反，他的思想太多，太散，太快，空有知识宽度，没有知识密度。河宽，水浅，流量还大，所以水面永远不平，全是情绪、自治和理由。

Take a young person who scrolls Douyin all day. They know a little about a lot of things — a bit of finance today, a bit of history tomorrow, some relationship advice the day after, then a bit about entrepreneurship; a little philosophy here, a little military affairs there. You'd think this person has a very broad information base. And yes — their river is very wide. But the problem is, the water is very shallow. They know a bit of everything but nothing deeply. So even a few small stones — Greed and Fear — are enough to churn the surface into chaos. One day they're fired up, the next day they're in tears. Then they become stubbornly certain: today they believe life should be this way, tomorrow they think it should be that way. It's not that these people lack thoughts — quite the opposite, they have too many thoughts, too scattered, too fast. They have the breadth of knowledge but none of the density. The river is wide, the water is shallow, and the flow is heavy, so the surface is never calm — nothing but emotions, Self-rationalization, and excuses.

---

> 再比如一个肿瘤学教授，他不必什么都懂，未必每个领域都看，但他在一个或两个方向上挖得很深。这种人的河，不必特别宽，但水很深。水深的时候，底下即使有石头，也不容易一下子把表面搅得乱七八糟。所以这种人通常不容易大起大落，不是因为他没有情绪，也不是因为他没有贪婪和恐惧，而是因为他的水够深。

Now take an oncology professor. They don't need to know everything or follow every field, but in one or two directions they've dug very deep. This person's river doesn't need to be especially wide, but the water is deep. When the water is deep, even if there are stones on the bottom, they can't easily churn the surface into chaos. So this kind of person doesn't usually experience wild highs and lows — not because they have no emotions, and not because they have no Greed or Fear, but because their water is deep enough.

---

> 再比如我的一个朋友，陈昌文老师，他微博1400万粉丝，他的信息面非常广，博览群书，看得多，知道得也多。一次背叛让他被骗了巨额财产，但他后来对人性很失望，慢慢不愿意接触人，也不再愿意把太多外界信息放进自己心里，他写自己的东西，写自己的生活。越来越像在主动缩减流量。这种人很有意思，他的河本来很宽，水也不浅，但他后来开始主动控制"水从哪里来，又流到哪里去"，所以他的表面看上去会更平了，不是因为世界安静了，是因为他不再让太多杂乱的水灌进来。

Then there's a friend of mine, a teacher named Chen Changwen. He has fourteen million followers on Weibo. His information range is extremely broad — he reads widely, sees a lot, knows a lot. A betrayal once cost him an enormous fortune. After that, he became deeply disillusioned with human nature and gradually withdrew from people. He stopped letting so much outside information into his inner world. He writes his own things, writes about his own life. More and more, it looks like he's deliberately reducing the flow. This kind of person is fascinating. His river was originally very wide, and the water wasn't shallow either. But later, he began to actively control *where the water comes from and where it goes*. So his surface appears calmer now — not because the world got quieter, but because he stopped letting so much turbulent water flood in.

---

> 所以同样是"思想像河"，不同的人完全是不同的样子：

So even though the metaphor is always "thoughts are like a river," different people are entirely different kinds of rivers:

---

> 有的人是宽而浅。
>
> 有的人是窄而深。
>
> 有的人宽而深，但流速太快。
>
> 有的人河本来很美，却因为流量太大，整条河都乱了。

> Some are wide but shallow.
>
> Some are narrow but deep.
>
> Some are wide and deep, but the current is too fast.
>
> Some had a beautiful river to begin with, but the flow became so heavy that the whole thing turned to chaos.

---

> 一个人想看清自己，第一步不是急着问自己贪婪什么、恐惧什么。第一步是先搞清楚——你是哪一种河。你是哪一种河。这个问题听起来很简单，但大多数人一生都没有认真想过。他们每天都在看水面：今天心情不好，今天很焦虑，今天突然很有动力，今天又突然什么都不想做。他们会以为问题在情绪，于是开始想办法控制情绪。有人去听励志课，有人去学心理学，有人去冥想，有人去找成功学。可很多人折腾了很久以后，还是会回到原来的状态。不是因为这些方法完全没有用，而是因为他们一直在水面上打转。水面本来就会动：

If you want to see yourself clearly, the first step is not to rush into asking what you're greedy for or what you fear. The first step is to figure out: *what kind of river are you?* What kind of river are you. The question sounds simple, but most people go their entire lives without ever seriously considering it. Every day, they watch the surface: today I'm in a bad mood, today I'm anxious, today I suddenly feel motivated, today I suddenly don't want to do anything. They assume the problem is their emotions, so they start trying to control their emotions. Some go to motivational seminars, some study psychology, some try meditation, some chase self-help gurus. But after all that effort, many of them end up right back where they started. Not because those methods are completely useless, but because they've been spinning on the surface the whole time. The surface will always move:

---

> 风一吹就动，水一多就动，流速一快就动。

> A gust of wind makes it move. Too much water makes it move. A faster current makes it move.

---

> 如果你只盯着水面，你永远会觉得问题很多，但当你退一步，看整条河的时候，你会发现很多事情其实非常简单。有的人焦虑，不是因为他太敏感，而是因为他的河太浅，一点点事情就能碰到河底。有的人反复纠结钻牛角尖，不是因为他想不明白，而是因为他的河太窄，所有问题都挤在一条很小的河道里打转。有的人一会儿热血、一会儿悲观，不是因为他性格矛盾，而是因为他的流量太大，信息像暴雨一样往心里灌，整条河根本没有慢下来过。有的人明明条件很好，却总觉得生活很乱，不是因为世界特别针对他，而是因为他的流速太快，水一直在冲，很多东西还没来得及沉下来，就已经被下一波水带走了。

If you only stare at the surface, you'll always feel like the problems are endless. But when you step back and look at the whole river, you'll find that many things are actually very simple. Some people feel anxious not because they're too sensitive, but because their river is too shallow — the smallest thing hits bottom. Some people go around in circles, trapped in overthinking, not because they can't figure things out, but because their river is too narrow — every problem gets crammed into one tiny channel and churns endlessly. Some people swing between passion and despair not because their personality is contradictory, but because their flow is too heavy — information pours into their heart like a downpour, and the whole river never gets a chance to slow down. Some people have every advantage and still feel like their life is a mess, not because the world has singled them out, but because their current is too fast — the water keeps rushing, and things haven't had time to settle before the next wave carries them away.

---

> 所以后来我越来越觉得，很多人把人生想得太复杂了。他们总想找到一个"答案"，一个能解决所有问题的方法。可实际上，很多问题在更早的地方就已经决定了。

So over time, I came to feel that most people make life far too complicated. They're always looking for "the answer" — one method that will solve everything. But in reality, many problems were already decided much further upstream.

---

> 决定你水面形状的，不是水面，而是河。
>
> 你如果不先看河，所有努力都可能用错地方。

> What determines the shape of your surface is not the surface — it's the river.
>
> If you don't look at the river first, all your effort may be aimed at the wrong place.

---

> 有人拼命往河里加水。以为知道更多就能解决问题，结果只是让水面更乱。

Some people frantically pour more water into their river, thinking that knowing more will solve their problems. All it does is make the surface more chaotic.

---

> 有人拼命想控制流速，可河太浅，再慢也一样乱。

Some people desperately try to slow the current, but the river is too shallow — even at a crawl, it's still turbulent.

---

> 有人试图堵住某一块水面，却没意识到真正的问题在河底。

Some people try to block off a patch of the surface, never realizing that the real problem lies on the riverbed.

---

> 所以后来我慢慢形成一个很简单的习惯：当我看到一个人状态很乱的时候，我不会马上去想他的情绪，我会先想一件事：他的河是什么样？是太窄，还是太浅？是水太多，还是流太快？因为只要把这一点看清楚，很多问题其实已经解决了一半。但当我继续往下想的时候，又出现了一个新的问题：

So over time, I developed a very simple habit: when I see someone whose life is in disarray, I don't immediately think about their emotions. I think about one thing first — *what does their river look like?* Is it too narrow, or too shallow? Is there too much water, or is it flowing too fast? Because once you see that clearly, half the problem is already solved. But when I kept thinking deeper, a new question appeared:

---

> 如果人的思想像河水一样不断流动。
>
> 那这些水到底流到哪里去了？

> If a person's thoughts flow like river water, constantly moving —
>
> then where does all that water actually go?

---

> 人与人之间当然会交换——你听我说一句话，我读你写的一本书，我们聊一个晚上，水会从一条河流进另一条河。但如果只是这样交换，其实很快就会消失。很多思想并不会只存在于两个人之间，有些思想会活得非常久，有的甚至可以活几百年。为什么？因为人类其实一直在做一件事：

Between people, of course there is exchange — you hear me say a sentence, I read a book you wrote, we talk through the night, and water flows from one river into another. But if it were only this kind of exchange, it would actually disappear very quickly. Many thoughts don't exist only between two people. Some thoughts live for a very long time — some even survive for hundreds of years. Why? Because humanity has actually been doing one thing all along:

---

> 给河水做容器。
>
> 最早的容器，是故事；后来是文字。
>
> 再后来，是书。
>
> 再后来，是艺术。

> Making containers for river water.
>
> The earliest container was the story. Then came the written word.
>
> Then came books.
>
> Then came art.

---

> 现在他叫大语言模型"ai"。这些东西其实都在做同一件事：把一条河里的水装起来。一本书，其实就是一段被保存下来的河流。一个人几十年的思考、一生的观察，如果只是在聊天中说完，很快就会消失，但一旦写进书里，这条河就被装进了一个容器。几百年以后，还会有人打开它，让那条河再次流进自己的心里。

Now the container is called a large language model — "AI." All of these things are actually doing the same work: capturing the water from a river. A book is really a stretch of river that has been preserved. A person's decades of thinking, a lifetime of observation — if it's only spoken aloud in conversation, it will vanish quickly. But once it's written into a book, that river has been poured into a container. Hundreds of years later, someone will still open it and let that river flow into their own heart once more.

---

> 艺术也是一样。
>
> 一幅画。
>
> 一段音乐。
>
> 一部电影。
>
> 其实都是某个人思想河流的一部分。
>
> 被凝固成了一个形状。

> Art is the same.
>
> A painting.
>
> A piece of music.
>
> A film.
>
> Each one is a fragment of someone's river of thought,
>
> solidified into a shape.

---

> 等别人靠近的时候，那条河就会重新流动。历史更是这样。历史不是一堆年份，历史其实是无数条河床的影子——无数人的思想、选择、欲望、恐惧，被时间压缩以后留下的河床。

When someone else draws close, the river begins to flow again. History is even more so. History is not a pile of dates. History is really the shadow of countless riverbeds — the thoughts, choices, desires, and fears of countless people, compressed by time into the beds they left behind.

---

> 所以后来我慢慢意识到一件事：人类之所以能够一直发展，不是因为每一代人都从零开始，而是因为前面那些河水并没有消失，它们被装进了容器。于是当一个人读书的时候，其实是在让几百年前的河水流进自己的河；当一个人看历史的时候，其实是在看那些已经干涸的河床；当一个人接触艺术的时候，其实是在触碰另一条河曾经的流动。

So gradually I came to realize something: the reason humanity has been able to keep advancing is not that each generation starts from zero, but that the rivers that came before never truly disappeared — they were poured into containers. When a person reads a book, they are really letting water from centuries ago flow into their own river. When a person studies history, they are really looking at riverbeds that have long since gone dry. When a person encounters art, they are really touching the flow that once moved through another river.

---

> 我的河面宽阔，水面平静，都来自于上古先贤的知识，不是凭空而来。很多人会嘲讽我想做"圣人"，我从来未曾想过，他们做的事情是开创创新，而我做的事情是继承发扬。我知道我得到了巨大的礼物，于是也欠下了巨额的债务。我有义务也有责任在离开这个世界之前，把我的河流汇聚成容器，交还给这个世界。哪怕他给出百分之一的利息，我得到人间价值。所以我自嘲我是个破产的人，正在还债，还债者是没有资格要求别人不抬价和等等你的。

My river's surface is broad and calm, and that comes from the knowledge of the ancient sages — it didn't appear out of thin air. Many people mock me for wanting to be a "sage." I have never thought any such thing. What the sages did was originate and innovate. What I do is inherit and carry forward. I know I have received an enormous gift, and with it I have incurred an enormous debt. I have both the obligation and the responsibility, before I leave this world, to gather my river into a container and give it back. Even if it returns just one percent in interest, that would be a life well-spent. So I joke that I'm a bankrupt man, repaying a debt — and a debtor has no right to ask others not to raise the price or to wait for him.

---

> 所以当我后来开始写东西的时候，我也在想一个问题：如果我把自己的河写进书里，是不是等于把水全部放走？是不是有一天，我的河会变干？后来我慢慢发现不是这样。因为当水被装进容器的时候，它并没有消失，它只是换了一种存在方式。书里的水，会流向别人，别人的河又会产生新的水，然后再流回来。思想从来不是单向的，它更像是一整个系统——无数条河在交换、在分流、在汇合、在冲刷彼此的河床。

So when I later started writing, I was also thinking about a question: if I write my river into a book, am I letting all the water out? Will my river one day run dry? Over time, I realized it doesn't work that way. Because when water is poured into a container, it doesn't vanish — it simply takes on a different form of existence. The water in a book flows to others, and their rivers produce new water, which flows back again. Thought is never one-directional. It's more like an entire system — countless rivers exchanging, branching, merging, scouring each other's beds.

---

> 所以当一个人开始表达的时候，他并不是在消耗自己的河水。恰恰相反，他是在让整条河开始真正流动。而河一旦开始高速流动，河底就会慢慢被冲刷，很多原来坚固的地方会被带走，很多原来看不见的地方会露出来，于是河会变深。但当河越来越深的时候，你也会开始看到一些以前看不到的东西——那些真正改变水流方向的东西，那些一直在下面，却很少被人看见的东西。

So when a person begins to express themselves, they are not draining their river. Quite the opposite — they are setting the whole river into true motion. And once a river begins to flow at speed, the riverbed starts to be scoured. Things that once seemed solid get carried away. Things that were once invisible are revealed. The river grows deeper. But as the river deepens, you also begin to see things you couldn't see before — the things that truly change the direction of the current, the things that have always been down there, yet are rarely seen.

---

> 那一天，在神农架那条小溪边，我还没有完全想明白这些。但我已经隐约意识到一件事：如果一个人真的想看清自己，光看水是不够的，光看河也不够。因为真正改变水流方向的东西，一直都在下面——在河底。

That day, beside that little stream in Shennongjia, I hadn't fully worked all of this out. But I had begun to sense something: if a person truly wants to see themselves clearly, it's not enough to look at the water. It's not even enough to look at the river. Because the things that truly change the direction of the current have always been below — on the riverbed.
