# Chapter 3: The Coin World | 第三章 · 硬币世界

> "迷茫""**抑郁**"的原因，
>
> 不是他不贪了，
>
> 而是贪婪在多次"**失败**""**挫折**"的打击下，
>
> 碎成恐惧，
>
> 堵塞了思想之河，
>
> 他暂时失去了"**行动力**"

The reason for "confusion" and "**depression**"
is not that he stopped being greedy,
but that his Greed, after being battered by repeated "**failures**" and "**setbacks**,"
shattered into Fear,
dammed up his river of thought,
and he temporarily lost his "**drive to act**."

---

> 硬币世界这一章很重要，也很难懂，看完就能明白，贪婪为何在失败后会变成恐惧、恐惧又是如何悄无声息的让我们对这个世界失去兴趣、迷茫、抑郁和痛苦。

The Coin World chapter is very important — and very hard to understand. Once you've read it through, you'll see why Greed turns into Fear after failure, and how Fear silently, imperceptibly drains us of interest in the world, leaving us lost, depressed, and in pain.

---

> 我一直是一个对古币特别感兴趣的人，除了货币属性，硬币在历史的很多时刻都象征的"天意"，这种天意的正义性，正是我看清自己恐惧贪婪的极其重要的一步，而是因为我越来越觉得，人心里的很多东西，和硬币很像。恐惧与贪婪，知行合一的知与行，一个硬币的两面永远同时存在，但你却无法同时看到。我最早意识到这一点，不是因为看书。而是因为我小时候真的做过一件很蠢又特别真实的关于硬币和自行车的故事。

I have always been someone with a particular fascination for old coins. Beyond their monetary value, coins have symbolized "the will of heaven" at many turning points in history. That sense of cosmic justice in a coin toss was a crucial step in my coming to see my own Fear and Greed clearly — because I increasingly felt that many things inside the human heart are a lot like a coin. Greed and Fear, the "knowing" and the "doing" of unity between knowledge and action — the two sides of a coin always exist at the same time, yet you can never see both at once. The first time I realized this wasn't from reading a book. It was because, as a child, I actually did something quite foolish — and utterly real — involving a coin and a bicycle.

---

> 12岁的时候爸爸去四川回来，送过我一枚熊猫纪念币，金色的，我一直以为那是真金币。可后来有一段时间，我特别想买一辆好一点的自行车，但我爸妈不同意，我自己又没有钱。于是我就开始盯着那枚硬币看。

When I was twelve, my father came back from a trip to Sichuan and gave me a gold-colored panda commemorative coin. I always believed it was real gold. But then came a period when I desperately wanted a nicer bicycle. My parents wouldn't agree, and I had no money of my own. So I started staring at that coin.

---

> 看着看着，我心里就慢慢冒出来一个念头：要不要把它卖了。

The longer I stared, the more a thought crept in: what if I sold it?

---

> 这个念头一出来，事情就开始有意思了，因为从那一刻开始，我心里其实已经同时站着两股力，一股是想要，一股是害怕。想要那辆车，想把"我喜欢"变成"我得到"，这是贪婪；可我也知道，这硬币是我爸送我的，拿去卖掉这件事，怕以后被骂，怕自己卖了之后又后悔，这是恐惧。

The moment that thought appeared, things got interesting — because from that point on, two forces were already standing inside me at the same time. One was wanting. The other was fearing. Wanting that bicycle, wanting to turn "I like it" into "I have it" — that was Greed. But I also knew the coin was a gift from my father. The thought of selling it made me afraid of getting scolded later, afraid I'd regret it afterward — that was Fear.

---

> 所以我开始"掷硬币"。

So I started flipping the coin.

---

> 我一开始给自己定一个规则：如果掷两次正面，我就去卖，结果第一次正面，第二次反面。于是我想，那就改规则为两次反面，结果又变成一正一反。然后我又想，那就换个规则，再来，再来，再来。

At first I set myself a rule: if I flip heads twice in a row, I'll go sell it. The first flip came up heads; the second, tails. So I thought, fine, I'll change the rule to two tails. Then it came up one heads, one tails. So I changed the rule again. And again. And again.

---

> 我不停地改规则，两次正面，不行。两次反面，不行。一正一反，也不行。总之，我一直摸不到那个让我"安心"的天意。到最后，我其实已经知道，真正难的从来不是硬币，难的是我自己。我不是在等一个答案，我是在等一个理由，和现在和我连麦的很多粉丝很类似，等一个可以让我把这件事做下去，同时又不用完全承认"这是我自己决定的"的理由。后来我又改了一次规则。我说，那就三次吧。如果掷出三次不连续相同，我就去卖。结果真的掷出来一正一反一正。

I kept changing the rules. Two heads — no good. Two tails — no good. One heads, one tails — still no good. No matter what, I could never land on the "divine verdict" that would put my mind at ease. By the end, I already knew: the hard part was never the coin. The hard part was me. I wasn't waiting for an answer. I was waiting for a reason — much like the fans who call in during my livestreams — a reason that would let me go through with it without having to fully admit: "This was my own decision." Then I changed the rule one more time. I said: three flips. If none of the three are the same in a row, I'll go sell it. And sure enough, I got heads, tails, heads.

---

> 于是我拿着那枚硬币，去卖了。可真正到了店里，人家一看，说这不是真金币。这是银币镀金。我当时特别沮丧，因为我前面折腾了那么久，反复掷，反复改规则，反复说服自己。结果最后换来的，甚至都不是我以为的那个东西。

So I took that coin and went to sell it. But when I actually got to the shop, the dealer took one look and said: this isn't real gold. It's a silver coin with gold plating. I was devastated. I'd agonized for so long — flipping over and over, changing the rules over and over, talking myself into it over and over — and in the end, what I got wasn't even what I thought it was.

---

> 但也就是在那个瞬间，我突然明白了一件事。我前面一直盯着硬币的正面和反面，觉得自己是在等一个"天意"。可真正决定我行动的，从来不是硬币，是我自己，更准确地说，是我心里那两股一直在打架的东西。

But right in that moment, I suddenly understood something. All along, I'd been staring at the heads and tails of the coin, thinking I was waiting for "heaven's will." But what actually drove my actions was never the coin — it was me. More precisely, it was those two forces inside me that had been fighting the whole time.

---

> 一股想要。
>
> 一股害怕。

> One that wanted.
>
> One that feared.

---

> 从那以后，我看到"硬币"这两个字，就很难再把它只当成一个物件看，他变成了我观测世界的一种角度，我叫小世界。很多年以后，我在电视剧少帅里看到张学良和杨宇霆、常荫槐那段旧事的时候，我一下就有感觉了。

From then on, whenever I saw the word "coin," I could no longer see it as just an object. It became a lens through which I observed the world — a Small World. Many years later, when I watched the TV series "The Young Marshal" and the episode about Zhang Xueliang's history with Yang Yuting and Chang Yinhuai, I felt an immediate resonance.

---

> 很多人讲那个故事，会把重点放在"到底掷了几次""到底是不是做过手脚""到底是不是天意"这些地方。我后来越来越觉得，那些都不重要。重要的是：他为什么需要一枚硬币。

Many people, when they tell that story, focus on "how many times was the coin actually flipped," "was it rigged," "was it really heaven's will." Over time, I came to feel that none of those details matter. What matters is: why did he need a coin in the first place?

---

> 人很多时候不是不知道自己想做什么。而是不愿意承担这一句：这是我自己决定的，于是我们就会给自己找很多理由，找一个看起来不是"我"的理由。

Most of the time, people aren't unaware of what they want to do. They just don't want to bear the weight of this one sentence: "This was my own decision." So they look for reasons — reasons that don't look like "me."

---

> 有的人找运气
>
> 有的人找形势
>
> 有的人找概率
>
> 有的人找一枚硬币

> Some people look for luck.
>
> Some people look for circumstance.
>
> Some people look for probability.
>
> Some people look for a coin.

---

> 可这些东西，很多时候都只是表面，真正压在下面的，其实还是那两股力。一股想往前，一股想往后，一股想得到，一股怕失去。

But most of the time, these are all surface. What's really pressing underneath is still those same two forces. One wants to move forward, one wants to pull back. One wants to gain, one fears losing.

---

> 所以后来我才越来越习惯用硬币去理解很多事情。很长一段时间，我特别喜欢去很多有清澈见底的许愿池的地方发呆。当我又在犹豫和顾虑的时候我会去到这里，我安静的坐在一边看着大家丢下属于的恐惧与贪婪，思考自己的代价与结构。

That's why I gradually got into the habit of using the Coin to understand many things. For a long time, I was especially drawn to places with crystal-clear wishing fountains, where I'd just sit and zone out. Whenever I was caught up in hesitation and doubt, I'd go to one. I'd sit quietly off to one side, watching everyone toss in their own Fear and Greed, while I thought about my own costs and structures.

---

> 当我站在一边旁观，看着他们丢下一枚枚硬币，一会欢呼雀跃，一会失落悲伤，一会一笑而过，一会反复尝试，我就会走进我的硬币世界，万家灯火那一刻就仿佛一枚硬币。

Standing off to the side, watching them throw in coin after coin — sometimes cheering, sometimes deflated, sometimes laughing it off, sometimes trying again and again — I'd slip into my Coin World, and in that moment, every lit window in the city seemed like a coin.

---

> 一面是贪婪。
>
> 一面是恐惧。

> One side is Greed.
>
> One side is Fear.

---

> 不是轮流来的，只是某一刻，哪一面朝上。你往前冲的时候，你以为自己是贪婪，其实恐惧也在。你也许怕苦，怕输，怕一辈子只是个普通人。你退下来的时候，你以为自己是恐惧，其实贪婪也还在，你也许还是想要，还是不甘心，还是舍不得，只是你更怕付那个代价。所以人真正的状态，从来不是"我只有贪婪"，也不是"我只有恐惧"。

They don't take turns. It's just that at any given moment, one side is facing up. When you charge forward, you think it's Greed driving you — but Fear is there too. Maybe you're afraid of hardship, afraid of losing, afraid of being ordinary your whole life. When you pull back, you think it's Fear holding you — but Greed is still there too. Maybe you still want it, still can't accept it, still can't let go. It's just that you're even more afraid of paying the cost. So a person's true state is never "I only have Greed" or "I only have Fear."

---

> 真实状态是：

The real state is:

---

> 贪婪和恐惧同时存在
>
> 只是某一刻，哪一边阻力更大。
>
> 哪一边阻力更小，水流就往哪边走。

> Greed and Fear exist simultaneously.
>
> It's just that at any given moment, one side has more resistance.
>
> Whichever side has less resistance — that's where the water flows.

---

> 到这里我们慢慢把前面"河底的石头"这件事想清楚。河底的石头并不一样，有些是大石头，有些是小碎石头。

Here we can begin to think more carefully about "the stones on the riverbed" from before. The stones on the riverbed are not all the same. Some are large stones, and some are small stones.

---

> 贪婪是大石头，它更显眼，更容易被看见，更容易直接改变水流。一个人想赚钱，想翻身，想成功，想证明自己，想得到某种未来，这些东西通常都很明显。你站在岸边，一眼就能看出来：哦，我想要这个。哦，我是冲这个去的。哦，我这么拼，是因为我太想拿到了。

Greed is the large stones. They're more conspicuous, easier to see, more likely to change the current directly. When someone wants to make money, wants to turn things around, wants to succeed, wants to prove themselves, wants to reach some future — these things are usually obvious. Standing on the bank, you can see it at a glance: oh, I want that. Oh, that's what I'm going after. Oh, I'm pushing this hard because I want it so badly.

---

> 所以我说，贪婪像大石头。这无关乎任何高级或者低级，而只是因为它对水流的影响，很容易被肉眼发觉，水冲过去，表面会立刻涌起。人也是这样。一个人贪婪很强的时候，动作快，情绪大，决策猛，你很容易看见。

That's why I say Greed is like large stones. This has nothing to do with being noble or base. It's simply that its effect on the current is easy to spot with the naked eye — the water rushes over, and the surface surges immediately. People are the same way. When someone's Greed is strong, they move fast, feel intensely, decide boldly — it's easy to see.

---

> 可恐惧不一样，恐惧更像小石头，小，碎，不显眼，更会藏。它很少说：我害怕。它更常说的是：再等等，还没准备好，更谨慎一点。不是不敢，只是想清楚一点。不是退缩，只是觉得现在不合适。所以人很容易感受到恐惧。但更难觉察恐惧。

But Fear is different. Fear is more like small stones — small, fragmented, inconspicuous, skilled at hiding. It rarely says: "I'm afraid." What it more often says is: "Let's wait a bit," "I'm not ready yet," "Let's be more careful." "It's not that I don't dare — I just want to think it through." "It's not retreat — I just don't think now is the right time." So people easily *feel* Fear. But it's much harder to *notice* Fear.

---

> 你会知道自己不舒服。会知道自己退了。会知道自己犹豫了。但你未必知道，那下面压着的，其实是怕。怕失败，怕丢脸，怕麻烦，怕白做，怕别人笑，怕承认自己其实没那么厉害，怕自己不过就是个普通人。

You'll know you're uncomfortable. You'll know you pulled back. You'll know you hesitated. But you might not know that underneath all of it, what's really pressing down is fear. Fear of failure, fear of embarrassment, fear of hassle, fear of wasted effort, fear of being laughed at, fear of admitting you're not as capable as you thought, fear that you're just an ordinary person after all.

---

> 所以后来我越来越觉得，人平时看见的，往往只是大石头，觉得自己很想要。很想赢，很想证明，可真正把人困住的，很多时候反而是那些细碎的小石头，它们平时不显眼，可它们会堆。

So over time, I came to feel that what people usually see are only the large stones. They feel like they really want something. Really want to win, really want to prove themselves. But what truly traps people, more often than not, are those small, fragmented stones. They're inconspicuous in normal times, but they pile up.

---

> 一颗怕失败，一颗怕丢脸，一颗怕白忙，一颗怕被拒绝，一颗怕别人看不起自己，一颗怕自己其实配不上自己想要的东西。平时看都不看，很多人在忽略中慢慢事情发展到不可收拾的地步，恐惧他堆成一座堰塞湖，这就是恐惧最可怕的地方。

One stone of fearing failure, one of fearing embarrassment, one of fearing wasted effort, one of fearing rejection, one of fearing being looked down on, one of fearing that you don't actually deserve what you want. Most of the time no one pays them any attention. Many people, through neglect, let things quietly deteriorate until it's beyond repair. The Fear piles up into a dammed lake — and that is the most terrifying thing about Fear.

---

> 贪婪让水面起浪
>
> 恐惧让河道堵塞
>
> 大石头把水顶起来
>
> 小石头把水堵住

> Greed makes the surface churn.
>
> Fear makes the channel clog.
>
> Large stones push the water up.
>
> Small stones block the water entirely.

---

> 很多人说看到《人选天选论》，不敢看，觉得自己无法行动不敢选了，这是典型的恐惧暂时形成堰塞湖的情况。所以很多人不是不想要，也不是没有动力，更不是完全没有贪婪。他只是被那些小石头一点一点堵住了，于是整个人会慢慢进入一种很奇怪的状态，表面上还活着，也还在想，偶尔也会激动，偶尔也会羡慕，偶尔也会说自己想改变。可真的轮到他往前走的时候，他不动了。

Many people say that when they encounter "A Philosophy of Choice and Cost," they don't dare read it, feeling they've already lost the ability to act and can't bring themselves to choose — this is a textbook case of Fear temporarily forming a dammed lake. So many people aren't lacking in desire, aren't lacking in drive, and aren't completely devoid of Greed. They've just been blocked, bit by bit, by those small stones. And so the whole person gradually drifts into a very strange state: on the surface they're still alive, still thinking, occasionally excited, occasionally envious, occasionally saying they want to change. But when it actually comes time to take a step forward — they don't move.

---

> 不是没有路，是河堵了。堰塞湖最麻烦的地方，不是浪大。恰恰相反，它看起来很平静。平静得像理性，像成熟，像看淡了，像没事，可你往下看，就会发现，根本不是。

It's not that there's no path. It's that the river is blocked. The most troublesome thing about a dammed lake isn't that the waves are big. Quite the opposite — it looks very calm. Calm enough to look like rationality, like maturity, like having made peace with things, like being fine. But if you look beneath the surface, you'll find it's nothing of the sort.

---

> 水还在，河也还在，可它已经不流了，水流不进来，更流不出去。河的沿岸迅速扩大，自己越恐惧、越学习，越学习就知道的越多，知道的越多，知道自己不知道就越多，越恐惧，河流越堵塞。

The water is still there, the river is still there, but it's no longer flowing. Water can't come in, and it certainly can't go out. The banks expand rapidly — the more afraid you become, the more you study; the more you study, the more you know; the more you know, the more you realize how much you don't know; the more you fear, the more the river clogs.

---

> 当一个人被恐惧的小石头堆住以后，最常见的状态不是疯狂，而是：拖延、自洽、麻木。对任何事情提不起兴趣，仿佛人生没有希望。

When a person's river is dammed by Fear's small stones, the most common state isn't frenzy. It's: procrastination, Self-rationalization, numbness. An inability to muster interest in anything, as if life holds no hope.

---

> 拖延，是因为流不动。
>
> 自洽，是因为要给"不动"找解释。
>
> 麻木，是因为堵久了，连自己都快忘了原来想去哪里。

> Procrastination — because the water can't flow.
>
> Self-rationalization — because you need to explain why you're not moving.
>
> Numbness — because once the dam has held long enough, you've almost forgotten where you were trying to go.

---

> 所以很多人迷茫，不是因为他没有目标，而是因为他的河已经被这些小石头堆住了。为何会有如此多的恐惧、贪婪去哪里了呢？这就是最可怕的循环：贪婪的大石头会碎、小石头却不会自己变成大石头。

So many people feel lost not because they lack a goal, but because their river has been dammed by these small stones. Where does all that Fear come from? Where did the Greed go? This is the most terrifying cycle of all: Greed's large stones can shatter — but small stones never reassemble into large ones on their own.

---

> 一个人年轻的时候，贪婪往往很大。想赢，想快，想翻身。想证明自己和别人不一样。所以那块石头很大。大石头也很多。水一冲过去，表面全是浪。可如果他一次次撞，一次次失败，一次次付代价，那块大石头是会碎的，碎成很多小石头。原本的"我想成功"，会慢慢碎成：我怕再输，我怕再丢脸，我怕白做，我怕别人笑我，我怕自己其实没那个命。

When a person is young, their Greed tends to be enormous. They want to win, want speed, want to turn everything around. They want to prove they're different from everyone else. So the stones are big. And there are a lot of them. The water rushes over them, and the whole surface is nothing but waves. But if they crash again and again, fail again and again, pay the cost again and again — those large stones will shatter. They break into many small stones. What was once "I want to succeed" slowly splinters into: I'm afraid of losing again, I'm afraid of embarrassment again, I'm afraid of wasted effort, I'm afraid of being laughed at, I'm afraid I just don't have what it takes.

---

> 所以很多人不是不贪了，是贪婪在一次次冲击之后，碎成了无数细小的恐惧。

So many people haven't stopped being greedy. Their Greed, after being battered over and over, has simply shattered into countless tiny fragments of Fear.

---

> 这也是为什么有些人年轻时很猛，后来越来越谨慎，越来越会说"算了"，别人会说他成熟了，但很多时候，不是成熟，是那块大石头被现实打碎了。说到这里，还有一个很多人会忽略的点。连我们开始观察自己、研究自己、想把这一套搞明白，这个行为本身，也不是中性的。很多人以为，自己开始研究内心，是因为理性，是因为成熟，是因为想变清醒。其实很多时候，也不是。有些人观察自己，是因为贪婪。

This is also why some people are fierce when they're young but grow increasingly cautious, increasingly prone to saying "forget it." Others call them mature — but much of the time, it isn't maturity. It's that the large stone was broken apart by reality. And here's another point that most people overlook: even the act of observing ourselves, studying ourselves, trying to figure all of this out — that itself is not neutral. Many people assume they started examining their inner world because they're rational, because they've matured, because they want clarity. But much of the time, that's not the reason either. Some people observe themselves because of Greed.

---

> 他想把这一套搞明白，然后赚钱
>
> 想把这一套搞明白，然后提前看懂别人
>
> 想把这一套搞明白，然后在世界里赢得更多
>
> 想把这一套搞明白，然后少走弯路，快点起来

> They want to figure this out so they can make money.
>
> They want to figure this out so they can read people in advance.
>
> They want to figure this out so they can win more from the world.
>
> They want to figure this out so they can take fewer wrong turns and rise faster.

---

> 这是贪婪在推。还有些人观察自己，是因为恐惧。他不是想赢，他只是怕以后再吃亏。怕以后再掉进同样的坑，怕以后突然出事，自己一点准备都没有。怕自己如果不把这些想明白，后面会出更大的问题。这是恐惧在拉。

That's Greed pushing. And some people observe themselves because of Fear. They're not trying to win — they just don't want to get burned again. They're afraid of falling into the same trap, afraid of something going wrong and being completely unprepared. They're afraid that if they don't think all of this through, even bigger problems await. That's Fear pulling.

---

> 所以后来我越来越明白：人很少因为纯粹的好奇而观察自己，这也就是如此多人反对和故意不理解我的原因了。很多时候，要么是贪婪推了一下，要么是恐惧拦了一下。这就意味着，连"观测"本身，也已经被硬币影响了。你不是站在一个完全客观的位置上看自己，你也是带着一枚正在翻转的硬币，低头去看河底。

So over time I came to understand: people rarely observe themselves out of pure curiosity — and this is precisely why so many people resist or deliberately refuse to understand me. Most of the time, either Greed gave a push, or Fear applied the brakes. Which means that even "observation" itself has already been influenced by the Coin. You're not standing in some perfectly objective position looking at yourself. You too are holding a coin that's mid-flip, bowing your head to peer at the riverbed.

---

> 这也是为什么，我后来越来越重视"怎么观察"。如果你想直观地看贪婪，其实不难。就看你最兴奋的时候，最冲的时候，最想证明自己的时候，最不甘心的时候，最想"拿到"的时候。

This is also why I came to place more and more importance on *how* to observe. If you want to see Greed directly, it's actually not hard. Just look at yourself when you're most excited, most charged up, most eager to prove yourself, most unwilling to accept things, most desperate to "get it."

---

> 因为大石头就是这样，它会直接把水顶起来，你不太可能看不见。可如果你想更中性地看恐惧，就没那么容易了。你不能在最激烈的时候看，你得在水面相对平的时候看。也就是在你没那么兴奋、也没那么慌的时候。

Because that's how large stones work — they push the water straight up, and it's almost impossible not to see it. But if you want to see Fear more neutrally, it's not so easy. You can't look during the most intense moments. You have to look when the surface is relatively still — when you're neither especially excited nor especially panicked.

---

> 比如跑完步以后，很累的时候，洗完澡以后，坐下来写东西的时候。那个时候，水慢一点，表面安静一点，你更容易看见那些平时看不见的小石头。所以我后来越来越觉得，贪婪最好在"浪起的时候"看。恐惧最好在"水平的时候"看。

For instance, after a run, when you're spent. After a shower. Sitting down to write something. In those moments the water slows a bit, the surface quiets a bit, and you're more likely to see those small stones that are normally invisible. So over time I came to feel: Greed is best observed "when the waves are high." Fear is best observed "when the water is flat."

---

> 写到这里，我反而越来越不觉得贪婪和恐惧是什么复杂的东西。它们太普通了，普通到每个人都有，普通到每一天都在发生。

By this point in my writing, I've actually come to feel less and less that Greed and Fear are anything complicated. They're utterly ordinary — so ordinary that everyone has them, so ordinary that they happen every single day.

---

> 你看到好机会的时候，它们一起出现
>
> 你爱一个人的时候，它们一起出现
>
> 你想离开的时候，它们一起出现
>
> 你决定创业的时候，它们一起出现
>
> 你准备认错的时候，它们一起出现

> When you see a great opportunity — they both appear.
>
> When you love someone — they both appear.
>
> When you want to leave — they both appear.
>
> When you decide to start a business — they both appear.
>
> When you're about to admit you were wrong — they both appear.

---

> 它们从来不是轮流来的，它们是同时都在，只是某一刻，哪一面更重，水流就往哪边去。所以人真正的问题，从来不是有没有贪婪，也不是有没有恐惧。

They never take turns. They're always both there. It's just that at any given moment, whichever side weighs more, that's where the water goes. So a person's real problem is never whether they have Greed, or whether they have Fear.

---

> 而是：当它们同时存在的时候，你看不看得见，哪一面硬币正在朝上。

It's this: when both exist at the same time, can you see which side of the Coin is facing up?

---

> 当我们思想初生，我们的思考如河流的前沿，到处都是大大的石头；当我们的人生走向老去，我们的河流只有小的石头了，甚至沙子了。所以我才很喜欢一句话：

When our minds are newly formed, our thinking is like the leading edge of a river — large stones everywhere. As our lives move toward old age, our river has only small stones left, perhaps even sand. That's why I've always loved this line:

---

> 天意从来高难问，况人情老矣乎，悲难诉。这才是人间灯火真真的样子。

> Heaven's will has always been high and hard to fathom — how much more so when the heart has grown old and the grief is beyond words. This is what the lights of the human world truly look like.

---

> 很多人一生都在讨论世界，讨论命运，讨论别人，讨论机会。却从来没有低头看过，自己手里那枚硬币，到底翻到了哪一面。而这，才是迷茫真正开始的地方。因为人为什么会迷茫。不是因为没有路。而是因为两面同时都在，自己却看不见。

Many people spend their entire lives discussing the world, discussing fate, discussing other people, discussing opportunities — yet never once bow their heads to look at the coin in their own hand and see which side it has landed on. And that is where confusion truly begins. Because why do people become lost? Not because there's no path. But because both sides are there at once, and they can't see either one.

---

> 那就是下一章了"我有一剑，斩灭迷茫"。

And that brings us to the next chapter: "I Have a Sword — To Cut Through Confusion."
